Redesigned website — home page and patient portal Rela Hospital · 2022
01

The problem

Rela Hospital's rapid growth had created a digital crisis. The website — built for a smaller institution — couldn't support the scale, services, or credibility the hospital had earned. Patients looking for doctors or departments couldn't find what they needed, and the trust signals patients depend on in healthcare simply weren't there.

The business was ready to digitize — appointment booking, lab tests, patient records — but the existing architecture couldn't hold any of it. The site was actively costing the hospital patients.

Before

  • 87% of users dropped off when searching for a doctor or department
  • 8 full-width homepage banners — crushing load time
  • Navigation was an uncategorized list of every hospital department
  • No appointment booking, no patient portal, no login
  • Widespread lack of trust — confirmed by 150+ survey responses

After

  • 87% boost in online patient conversions
  • 3.87× overall website traffic increase
  • Self-serve appointment booking via authenticated patient portal
  • Navigation rebuilt around user intent, not hospital org structure
  • Trust signals, credentials, and doctor profiles front and center
02

Research & discovery

Before designing anything, I needed to understand who was actually visiting the site and what they needed at that moment. I ran a website audit, usability studies, user interviews, stakeholder interviews, and a competitor analysis.

The most important insight: not all hospital website visitors are the same. A person in an emergency researches differently from someone doing weeks of research before a planned surgery. The existing site treated every visitor identically — and failed most of them.

I mapped three distinct user archetypes based on traffic data and intent, each requiring a fundamentally different information hierarchy and journey through the site.

The rapid responder

A caretaker or patient in an active emergency. They're nearby, they're urgent, and they need to reach the right place fast. Every extra click costs them.

Primary pages

Doctor profiles → Appointment booking → Department pages

75%+ intent to visit the hospital

The diagnosis detective

Researching a diagnosis — their own or a family member's. They need credibility: doctor credentials, treatment detail, patient stories, and proof the hospital can handle their specific condition.

Primary pages

Treatments & conditions → Doctor profiles → About / awards

<30% intent — the right content converts them

The wellness seeker

Health-conscious, proactively reading about symptoms, seasonal illness, and healthy living. Low conversion intent, but high volume — and a long-term brand-building opportunity.

Primary pages

Blogs → Clinical articles → Seasonal health content

<10% direct conversion — high SEO value

Design challenge

“One website. Three completely different users with different urgency, different needs, and different trust thresholds. How do you design a single navigation and content system that works for all three — without overwhelming any of them?”

03

Three pivots that shaped the design

This wasn't a cosmetic refresh — the core architecture had to change. Three decisions had the highest impact on outcomes.

Pivot 01

Navigation rebuilt around intent, not org structure

The old navigation was a raw dump of every hospital department — no hierarchy, no categorization, no empathy for how a patient actually thinks. Someone searching for a cardiologist shouldn't have to know what a cardiothoracic department is called internally.

I rebuilt the navigation around three user intents: find information, take action, and return to something. Departments were grouped by symptom and body system — not hospital org chart. CTAs were moved to the header and made persistent. The result was a menu that spoke patient language, not hospital administration language.

This structural change alone was expected to significantly reduce customer support overhead for navigation-related queries — one of the top complaint categories before the redesign.

Pivot 02

Trust as a design material

The research made one thing undeniable: 150+ survey responses showed a widespread lack of trust in the existing site experience. In healthcare, trust is not a nice-to-have — it's the conversion mechanism. Patients don't book appointments at hospitals they don't believe in.

I treated trust as a first-class design material throughout. Doctor profiles were elevated with credentials, experience, and patient-facing language. Accreditations, awards, and infrastructure were brought out of buried “About” pages and placed on high-traffic pages. The content tone shifted from institutional to human — from “Rela Hospital provides…” to “We are here for you.”

The 8-banner homepage was stripped back to a focused, fast-loading page with clear hierarchy — giving users a reason to stay and a path forward.

Pivot 03

Authenticated transactions — a new product layer

The hospital's goal was to digitize operations — online appointments, lab test bookings, patient records. But putting transactions on a public website without authentication was both a trust risk and a data risk. I designed a patient portal with a login layer that gated all transactional features.

This wasn't just security infrastructure — it was a UX decision. A logged-in user gets their data remembered, their appointment history surfaced, and a richer visual experience. It gave returning patients a reason to come back to the website rather than calling reception. And it gave the hospital a CRM foundation: a relationship with each patient that extended beyond a single visit.

Form design across the entire site was also unified — every form followed the same layout pattern, reducing cognitive load as users moved from booking an appointment to scheduling a lab test. Consistency in forms directly signals institutional reliability, which was the very thing patients said they were missing.

05

Reflections

In healthcare, trust is the conversion mechanism

You can't optimize your way to conversions on a hospital website if users don't believe in the institution. Every design decision — content tone, credential placement, form consistency — was ultimately a trust decision. Learning to design for trust as a primary outcome, not a secondary consideration, changed how I approached the entire project.

One site can't be one experience

The biggest unlock in this project was building user archetypes around intent and urgency — not just demographics. Designing for a rapid responder and a wellness seeker simultaneously required a navigation and content system flexible enough for both. That tension made the final architecture sharper and more useful than anything I would have designed for one user type alone.

Persuasion is a design skill

Redesigning a live hospital website meant challenging decisions that stakeholders had strong opinions about. Getting buy-in for the navigation overhaul, the content tone shift, and the decision to gate transactions behind authentication all required communicating design rationale in terms of patient outcomes and business goals — not design principles. The research data made those conversations possible.

Structured research unlocks scale

On a project this large — touching navigation, content, transaction flows, and a new product layer — structured research wasn't a phase, it was the load-bearing foundation. Every significant design decision traced back to a specific finding. That discipline is what made it possible to move fast and confidently on a project with this much scope.
